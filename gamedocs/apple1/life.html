Conway's Game of Life

John Conway, 1970 — Apple I 6502 machine code by Larry Nelson

Conway's Game of Life — a cellular automaton where simple rules produce complex, evolving patterns. This Apple I version asks for your name, uses it as a seed to generate a random starting pattern, then runs the simulation automatically. Watch cells live, die, and reproduce generation by generation.

Getting started

  1. The game loads via turbo tape and displays "CONWAY'S GAME OF LIFE".
  2. Type your full name and press Return. The name is used as a seed — different names produce different starting patterns.
  3. The simulation begins automatically. Live cells appear as * on the grid.

The rules

Each cell on the grid is either alive or dead. Every generation, all cells update simultaneously based on their eight neighbours:

ConditionResult
Live cell with fewer than 2 neighboursDies (underpopulation)
Live cell with 2 or 3 neighboursSurvives
Live cell with more than 3 neighboursDies (overpopulation)
Dead cell with exactly 3 neighboursBecomes alive (reproduction)

What to watch for

Pattern typeBehaviour
Still lifesStable shapes that never change (blocks, beehives)
OscillatorsShapes that cycle between states (blinkers, toads)
SpaceshipsShapes that move across the grid (gliders)
MethuselahsSmall patterns that take many generations to stabilise

Tips

  • Try different names. Each name seeds a different random pattern — some produce more interesting evolution than others.
  • Short names tend to produce sparser patterns; longer names fill more of the grid.
  • Most patterns stabilise into a mix of still lifes and oscillators after enough generations.
  • Edge effects. On the Apple I's finite grid, cells at the border behave differently than on an infinite plane.

Apple I keyboard

Type your name and press Return. The simulation then runs unattended.
